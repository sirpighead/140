Introduction

The modern conflict in Ukraine began with Russia's takeover of Crimea in 2014, and the uprisings in Ukraine's Donbas Region. However, tensions between the two countries have been high ever since the fall of the Soviet Union, and Ukrainians have been fighting for their independence since before World War II (Cengel).

In the late 18th century, the Russian Empire took over much of western and southern Ukraine, including modern day Crimea. In the 19th century, the region saw a "reawakening" of culture, education, and history, leading to a sense of nationalism and yearn for independence (BBC). These movements would culminate following the collapse of the empire in the early 20th century.

20th Century

Pre-WWII

After the fall of Tsarist Russia in the 1917 revolution, a council was created in Kyiv called the Central Rada, and the Ukrainian People's Republic was founded. This marked the beginning of the Ukrainian Independence movement, and is often overlooked by Russian scholars, including Putin in his 2021 essay "On the Historical Unity of Russians and Ukrainians". In doing so, Putin deliberately "overlooks the country's sovereignty" to further Russia's control over Ukraine (Dobczansky, Cengel). Unfortunately for the Ukrainian nationalists, this independence was shortlived.

In 1922, the Soviets regained control over most of Ukraine, establishing the Ukrainian Soviet Socialist Republic, with the western third becoming a part of Poland. In the early 1930s, Holodomor - the Great Famine, and intense cultural suppression take place. Holodomor was the deliberate act of Soviet policies to attack farmers for opposing mandates of collectivisation. This targetted atrocity cost the lives of around 3.9 million people - approcimately 13% of Ukrainians population at the time. In addition to this, the Soviets waged a war on Ukrainian culture and language (BBC, Cengel). The "Russification" was an attempt to subdue the history and sense of independence that Ukrainians had, to make them more complacent and easy to control.

WWII onward

Moreover, during World War II over 5 million Ukrainians died fighting Nazi Germany, and the large majority of the 1.5 million Jews in Ukraine are killed (BBC). However, historians do often note that Ukrainian nationalists often collaborated with Nazi Germany in rebellion against Soviet rule. Many of the largest massacres of the Holocaust took place in Ukraine, and local collaborators helped the Nazis murder between 70,000 and 100,000 people at Babyn Yar (a ravine on the outskirts of Kyiv). Many Ukrainians saw the Nazis as a way out from under the thumb of the U.S.S.R., a regime that had practically attempted a Ukrainian genocide just a decade prior. While it is important to acknowledge the country's problematic history with the Nazi's and the Holocaust, it is not just to use that as justification for the invasion of Ukraine and murdering of civilians, as Putin is doing (Cengel).

After World War II, armed resistance to Soviet rule continues until 1954, where the capturing of the last commander in the Ukrainian Insurgent Army marks the end. However, with the fall of the Soviet Union in 1991, Ukraine finally declares indpendence from Russia and begins the process of democratization. Hundreds of thousands of Crimeans can finally return to their homeland after their mass-deportation by Stalin in the 1950s. The country is unstable, however, in the infant years of its indpendence. The new government grapples with fierce political battles between pro-Russian and pro-Western candidates, as well as severe economic hardship following the 2008 recession (BBC). Domestic and foreign tensions rise all thoughout the early 21st century, and in late 2013 massive protest breaks out over the abandoning of an agreement with the EU. So begins the current era of the Russian-Ukrainian conflict.

Current Events

The Russian invasion of Ukraine began with the re-annexing of Crimea in March of 2014. Crimea is a very strategic region, as it holds most of the Ukrainian coastline on teh Mediterranean, as well as rich oil and natural gas deposits. A month later, Pro-Russian revolutionaries in the eastern Ukrainian region of Donbas stage an armed rebellion, backed with Russian funding and equipment. Since then, over 14,000 Ukrainian lives have been lost in the border conflict in Donbas.

The most recent events in Ukraine began in late February of 2022 with Russia's blatant ground and air invasion into the Northern and Eastern borders. While using de-nazification and historical unity as justifcation for the invasion, Putin simultaneously tramples the human rights of tens of millions of Ukrainians as his armies target civilian population centers and infrastructure. Russian forces have committed numerous war crimes through this invasion, and they need to be held accountable. Unfortunately, Russia either controls too much of, or is not a member of, international organizations like the International Court of Justice, or the International Criminal Court. So prosecution of Russian war criminals would be very difficult.
